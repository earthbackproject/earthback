### greywater

**Post GW-1** | `greywater` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Greywater ‚Äî water from sinks, showers, and laundry ‚Äî makes up roughly 65% of household wastewater. It's not sewage. In most temperate climates, it contains enough nutrients and organic matter to feed landscape plants without any treatment beyond a simple mulch basin. A branched drain system distributes laundry water directly to shrubs and trees, requires no pumps or electricity, and costs less than $200 to install. Most jurisdictions allow it for subsurface irrigation. Check your local code, then stop sending perfectly good water to the sewage plant.

**Post GW-2** | `greywater` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
The most common greywater system failure isn't clogged pipes ‚Äî it's plant toxicity from high-sodium laundry detergents. Conventional laundry detergent contains sodium-based surfactants that build up in soil and degrade its structure over time. The fix is simple: switch to sodium-free soap (plant-based castile soap, or laundry products specifically labeled greywater-safe). That one product swap makes the difference between a greywater system that kills your soil and one that builds it. Your soap choice is a landscape design decision.

### water-harvesting

**Post WH-1** | `water-harvesting` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
A swale is a level trench dug on contour across a slope. When it rains, runoff collects in the swale instead of flowing downhill, giving it time to infiltrate. The berm on the downslope side is planted with trees and shrubs whose roots access the stored moisture. Over time, the swale becomes a linear food forest ‚Äî each one adding land productivity where there was previously just erosion. The design cost is zero if you understand your contour lines. The water is already falling; you're just deciding where it goes.

**Post WH-2** | `water-harvesting` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Brad Lancaster's rule of thumb for earthworks: start with the smallest intervention that captures the most water, highest on the landscape, and work your way down. That means micro-swales and rock mulch before you excavate ponds. Every small intervention slows runoff and increases infiltration, which changes what's possible for the interventions below it. This sequencing principle ‚Äî slow, spread, sink before you store ‚Äî shapes every decision about earthworks on a site and keeps you from building infrastructure that works against itself.

### natural-dyeing

**Post ND-1** | `natural-dyeing` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Mordanting ‚Äî treating fiber with a mineral salt before dyeing ‚Äî is what makes natural dye permanent. Without a mordant, most plant dyes wash out within a few cycles. Alum (aluminum sulfate) is the most common mordant: non-toxic, widely available, and compatible with most dye plants. Iron mordant shifts colors toward gray-green and "saddens" hues. Copper brightens them. The mordant is not the dye ‚Äî it's the chemical bridge between the dye molecule and the fiber. Understanding mordants turns a hit-or-miss craft into a reproducible process.

**Post ND-2** | `natural-dyeing` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Indigo fermentation vats are one of the most distinctive processes in natural craft. Indigo pigment is insoluble in water ‚Äî it won't dye anything in its raw form. A fermentation vat creates an alkaline, oxygen-depleted environment that converts indigo to its soluble "leuco" form. You dip the fiber, pull it out, and watch it turn from yellow-green to blue as it oxidizes in the air. The color isn't in the vat ‚Äî it forms on the fiber as it meets oxygen. Every serious natural dyer maintains a living indigo vat the way a baker maintains a sourdough starter.

### weaving

**Post WV-1** | `weaving` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Every woven fabric is the intersection of two thread systems: the warp (set on the loom under tension before weaving begins) and the weft (passed through the warp on a shuttle). The warp determines the structure and length of the cloth. The weft determines the pattern, texture, and width. The relationship between them ‚Äî how often the weft goes over or under each warp thread ‚Äî is called the weave structure, and it determines everything: drape, durability, stretch, appearance. Plain weave, twill, satin, and tapestry are not aesthetic choices first. They're structural engineering.

**Post WV-2** | `weaving` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Traditional weaving communities developed their patterns as information systems ‚Äî recording kinship, status, territory, cosmology, and seasonal knowledge in cloth that traveled with the wearer. Andean textiles, West African kente, Japanese kasuri, and Scandinavian folk weaving each encode regional knowledge in their pattern grammar. Learning to read a textile tradition is learning to read a compressed archive of how a community understood itself and its world. That knowledge is worth preserving with more urgency than most people realize ‚Äî the weavers who hold it are not being replaced.

### basketry

**Post BK-1** | `basketry` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
A well-made basket uses only the material it needs. There's no joining compound, no fastener, no filler. Every strand is structural. The tension of one element against another is what holds the form ‚Äî coiling uses wrapped bundles; plaiting uses interlaced flat strips; twining uses twisted pairs of weavers that grip the stakes between them. The craft is essentially the same across every traditional culture that developed it independently: take a flexible long material and organize it against a rigid short material until the tension creates a container. Simple principle. Infinite variation.

**Post BK-2** | `basketry` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Sourcing your own basketry material changes the practice entirely. Black ash for Northeast American splint baskets, willow for British and European stake-and-strand, longleaf pine needles for Southern coiling, cattail for Midwestern plaiting ‚Äî every bioregion has its traditional basketry materials, and most of them can still be harvested from healthy populations. Managing a small willow coppice or cattail marsh for basketry creates a relationship with land that intensifies your attention to seasonal cycles, plant health, and long-term succession. The basket becomes a record of the place it came from.

### pottery-ceramics

**Post PT-1** | `pottery-ceramics` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Clay is the decomposed residue of feldspathic rock ‚Äî essentially ancient geology made plastic by water. What we call "throwing" is the manipulation of a clay body on a spinning wheel: centrifugal force opens the mass, and the hands compress and thin the walls from inside and outside simultaneously. The moment the clay dries to leather-hard, the walls become rigid enough to trim but not yet fired. Then bisque firing drives out all remaining moisture and organic matter. Then glaze, then high fire. The finished pot has passed through more distinct states of matter than almost any other object in everyday life.

**Post PT-2** | `pottery-ceramics` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Wood firing produces results that gas and electric kilns can't replicate. Flame and ash interact directly with the clay surface during long, high-temperature firings in anagama or noborigama kilns ‚Äî natural ash deposits melt onto surfaces, creating glaze effects that couldn't be planned. The unpredictability is the point. A wood firing is a collaboration between the potter, the fuel, the kiln's draft, the weather, and the position of each piece in the chamber. Communities of potters often fire together because loading, stoking, and attending a 72-hour firing requires multiple people and generates more meaning than any individual studio process.

### blacksmithing

**Post BS-1** | `blacksmithing` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Steel is iron with a small percentage of carbon ‚Äî typically 0.2% to 1.5%. Too little carbon and the metal is soft, won't hold an edge, and can't be hardened. Too much and it becomes brittle. Traditional blacksmiths developed intuition for carbon content by watching color during heating ‚Äî each color from black heat to white heat corresponds to a temperature range, and the temperature at which you work determines what's possible. This color-temperature-carbon relationship is the core literacy of the craft. Modern metallurgy quantified it; smiths were working with it empirically for thousands of years before that.

**Post BS-2** | `blacksmithing` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
A functioning forge, anvil, and set of hammers can produce almost every tool and fastener a natural building project needs ‚Äî hinges, hooks, bolts, brackets, door hardware, garden tools, wood-splitting wedges, chisels. Community blacksmiths historically supplied all of these. A well-maintained anvil lasts centuries; a traditional coal or propane forge can be built from basic materials. The barrier to entry for community-scale blacksmithing is mostly knowledge, not equipment cost ‚Äî the knowledge is well-documented and increasingly taught again as repair culture revives the craft.

### market-gardening

**Post MG-1** | `market-gardening` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Permanent raised beds on a no-till market garden don't get walked on. That single rule ‚Äî no foot traffic on the growing surface ‚Äî preserves the soil structure that makes intensive production possible. Compacted soil loses pore space; pore space is where oxygen, water, and roots live. On a permanent bed system, the soil never resets to compacted baseline. After 3-5 years of adding organic matter without tillage, a well-managed bed has structure that no amount of rototilling could replicate. The bed is the long-term investment; everything grown in it is interest.

**Post MG-2** | `market-gardening` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Succession planting turns a 4-foot bed into a year-round production unit. Radishes finish in 25 days ‚Äî sow a new bed every two weeks and you have continuous harvest. Lettuce bolts in heat ‚Äî transplant cold-weather varieties in fall and spring, direct sow heat-tolerant varieties for summer. Every time a crop finishes, the bed gets a thin layer of compost and a new transplant or direct seeding within 24 hours. Bare soil is a missed production window. The best market gardeners think of their beds as production schedules, not growing spaces.

### microgreens

**Post MG2-1** | `microgreens` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Microgreens are harvested at the cotyledon stage ‚Äî 7 to 14 days after germination depending on species. At that stage, studies have found concentrations of certain vitamins and phytonutrients 4 to 40 times higher than in mature plants of the same species. This isn't marketing; it's because the seedling is mobilizing its full nutrient reserves from the seed to fuel early growth. You're harvesting at the moment of maximum nutritional density. The tradeoff is yield: microgreens produce grams per tray, not pounds per bed. They're a supplement to diverse growing, not a replacement for it.

**Post MG2-2** | `microgreens` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
The two variables that separate mediocre microgreens from excellent ones are blackout time and air circulation. During the first 3-5 days, keeping trays in darkness and under weight (a heavy tray on top) drives etiolation ‚Äî the seedlings stretch toward where they expect light to be, producing long, tender stems. Then full light triggers chlorophyll development and rapid leaf expansion. After trays come into light, air circulation prevents damping off, which is the main failure mode ‚Äî a fungal collapse that takes out a tray overnight. A small fan running continuously is the difference between a 95% and a 50% success rate.

### aquaponics

**Post AQ-1** | `aquaponics` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Aquaponics closes a nutrient loop: fish produce ammonia-rich waste, bacteria convert it first to nitrite and then to nitrate, plants uptake the nitrate as fertilizer and clean the water, which returns to the fish. The bacterial colony ‚Äî the nitrifying biofilm ‚Äî is what makes the system work. It grows on any surface (grow bed media, filter material, tank walls) and takes 4-6 weeks to establish from scratch. Before the colony is mature, the system is in "cycling" mode and fish waste accumulates faster than it's processed. Most aquaponics failures happen in the first 6 weeks because the grower doesn't understand what's being waited for.

**Post AQ-2** | `aquaponics` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
The raft (DWC) and media bed are the two dominant small-scale aquaponics designs. Raft systems float styrofoam panels with plant net cups directly over fish effluent ‚Äî simple, high plant density, great for leafy greens. Media bed systems fill a raised bed with expanded clay or gravel, flood and drain it with fish water ‚Äî more complex, but handles fruiting plants (tomatoes, peppers) better because roots have physical support. Many mature systems combine both: raft for greens, media bed for fruiting crops, with the fish tank feeding both. The fish-to-plant ratio determines how hard the nitrogen cycle has to work.

### beekeeping

**Post BEE-1** | `beekeeping` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
A healthy honeybee colony at peak summer has 50,000 to 80,000 bees, a single laying queen producing up to 1,500 eggs per day, and enough foragers to visit 2 million flowers to produce a single pound of honey. The colony is not a collection of individuals doing simple tasks ‚Äî it's a superorganism making collective decisions through pheromone communication, waggle dances, and quorum sensing. Understanding the colony's seasonal logic (build up in spring, swarm to reproduce, hunker down in winter, requeen when the old queen fails) is more useful for beekeeping than memorizing management techniques.

**Post BEE-2** | `beekeeping` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Varroa destructor mites are the primary reason honeybee colonies fail in most of the world today. The mite reproduces in capped brood cells, feeds on developing bees, and transmits viruses that weaken the colony over multiple brood cycles. A colony can look healthy for months while varroa populations build to a threshold that causes collapse. Monitoring mite levels with an alcohol wash or sugar roll every 4-6 weeks is the single most important management practice for keeping bees alive. Treatment thresholds, timing, and method depend on the season and the beekeeper's philosophy ‚Äî but ignoring mite counts is how most new beekeepers lose their first colony.

### vermicomposting

**Post VC-1** | `vermicomposting` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Red wigglers (Eisenia fetida) are not soil earthworms ‚Äî they're litter-dwelling species that live at the surface in decomposing organic matter rather than deep in mineral soil. This is why they thrive in a worm bin and die if you put them in a garden bed. They process organic material into castings that are richer in available nutrients than conventional compost ‚Äî particularly in calcium, nitrogen, and beneficial microbial activity. A pound of red wigglers can process half a pound of food scraps per day. A healthy bin with 2 pounds of worms handles the food waste of a household of two people.

**Post VC-2** | `vermicomposting` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Worm castings work differently from fertilizer. They don't just supply nutrients ‚Äî they supply a microbial community and humic acids that improve how nutrients are held and released in soil. In studies, plants grown in soil amended with castings show better germination rates, faster growth, and improved disease resistance compared to plants grown with equivalent synthetic fertilizer. The castings are building microbial infrastructure that outlasts any single application. A 10% casting amendment in potting mix is the standard recommendation; above 30%, some growers see diminishing returns as the biology shifts.

### fermentation

**Post FM-1** | `fermentation` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Wild lacto-fermentation ‚Äî the kind that makes sauerkraut, kimchi, and naturally fermented pickles ‚Äî uses only salt and time. The salt creates an environment hostile to pathogenic bacteria but hospitable to lactobacillus, which is already present on the vegetable surface. As lactobacillus ferments the sugars, it produces lactic acid, which drops the pH and preserves the food. No vinegar. No heat. No added cultures. The microbes doing the work are native to the vegetable and the fermentation environment. This is preservation without external inputs ‚Äî the food preserves itself.

**Post FM-2** | `fermentation` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Sourdough is a managed ecosystem of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria living in a flour-and-water paste. The yeast produces CO‚ÇÇ (leavening) and flavor compounds; the bacteria produce lactic and acetic acids (sourness and shelf stability). The ratio between the two organisms, and between lactic and acetic acid, is controlled by hydration, temperature, and feeding schedule. A stiff, cold, infrequently fed starter favors more acetic acid (sharper, more vinegary). A loose, warm, frequently fed starter favors lactic acid (milder, more yogurt-like). The baker's job is managing the culture, not just baking the bread.

### agroforestry

**Post AF-1** | `agroforestry` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Silvopasture ‚Äî integrating trees into pasture ‚Äî is one of the most carbon-rich land uses available to farmers. Trees sequester carbon in wood, roots, and soil organic matter while producing timber, fruit, nuts, or fodder. Animals graze between the tree rows, their manure fertilizing the trees, and their browsing managing competing vegetation. Studies show silvopasture systems sequester 3-10 tonnes of CO‚ÇÇ equivalent per hectare per year ‚Äî more than any other agricultural practice. The trees don't compete with the pasture in a well-designed system; they complete it.

**Post AF-2** | `agroforestry` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Alley cropping puts annual crops in the light corridors between rows of trees or shrubs. The trees provide wind protection, modify microclimate, and (if nitrogen-fixing species are chosen) contribute fertility through leaf litter and root exudates. The crops provide income and food during the years before the trees mature. The system transitions over decades: high annual production early, high perennial production later, without ever having a fallow or unproductive phase. The design challenge is choosing tree spacing, species, and orientation so that light competition becomes a feature ‚Äî providing cool-season shade for heat-sensitive crops ‚Äî rather than a limitation.

### holistic-grazing

**Post HG-1** | `holistic-grazing` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Allan Savory's insight about planned grazing was that livestock ‚Äî when managed like wild herds ‚Äî can be a restoration tool rather than a degradation tool. Wild grazers moved in dense, mobile herds driven by predator pressure. They grazed intensively and briefly in any one place, then moved on and didn't return for months. Their impact ‚Äî hoof action, dung, urine, saliva on grazed plants ‚Äî and the long recovery period that followed built the deep grassland soils that once covered a third of Earth's land surface. Managed holistic grazing attempts to replicate this pattern: high density, short duration, long rest.

**Post HG-2** | `holistic-grazing` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
The recovery period is the most important and most neglected variable in grazing management. Conventional continuous grazing keeps animals on the same paddock indefinitely, selectively removing the plants they prefer and allowing less palatable species to dominate. Planned grazing gives each paddock a rest period long enough for grazed plants to fully regrow ‚Äî typically 60 to 120 days depending on climate and season. During that rest, soil biology recovers, plant root reserves rebuild, and the next grazing impact lands on fully recovered vegetation. The number of paddocks in rotation determines how long each paddock rests. More paddocks, longer rest, better outcome.

### forest-bathing

**Post FB-1** | `forest-bathing` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
The Japanese practice of shinrin-yoku ‚Äî "forest bathing" ‚Äî is now supported by a substantial body of research. Time spent in forested environments measurably lowers cortisol levels, blood pressure, and heart rate. It also increases natural killer cell activity ‚Äî a component of immune function that is suppressed by chronic stress. The mechanism appears to involve phytoncides: volatile organic compounds released by trees, particularly conifers, that humans inhale passively during a forest walk. You don't have to do anything. You don't have to meditate or exercise. The forest does the work. Slow presence is the whole practice.

**Post FB-2** | `forest-bathing` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
The difference between a walk in the woods and forest bathing is attention and time. Forest bathing is typically 2-4 hours in the same small area ‚Äî not a hike to cover distance, but a slow, open-ended sensory presence in one place. Studies comparing 15-minute and 2-hour exposures show the longer duration produces significantly greater physiological effects. The practice asks you to use all senses, move slowly, and follow curiosity without destination or goal. In a culture organized around productivity and efficiency, that instruction is harder than it sounds. The difficulty is precisely the practice.

### open-source

**Post OS-1** | `open-source` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
The Open Source Ecology project has developed open-source blueprints for 50 industrial machines ‚Äî tractor, cement mixer, wind turbine, 3D printer, laser cutter, hydraulic press ‚Äî designed to be buildable from commercially available parts for a fraction of their retail price. The Global Village Construction Set is the full kit: everything a small community needs to build a sustainable civilization from scratch. Every design is documented, shared freely, and improved collaboratively. This is what distributed manufacturing looks like ‚Äî not Amazon delivering to your door, but communities building their own tools from shared knowledge.

**Post OS-2** | `open-source` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Open-source hardware documentation is most valuable when it captures not just the final design but the failure history. Why did version 1 break? What did version 2 change and why? What are the known limitations of version 3? This context ‚Äî usually stripped out of finished product documentation ‚Äî is the knowledge that lets the next builder improve rather than repeat. The best open-source hardware projects have detailed build logs, failure documentation, and community forums where problems are diagnosed publicly. The documentation is as much the product as the hardware.

### natural-swimming

**Post NS-1** | `natural-swimming` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
A natural swimming pool works without chlorine by using a planted regeneration zone ‚Äî typically 50% of the total water surface ‚Äî filled with submerged and emergent aquatic plants whose roots and associated microbes filter and clarify the water biologically. The swimming zone stays clean because the water circulation draws it continuously through the plant zone. The system takes 2-3 seasons to establish biological maturity; in the first year, algae outcompetes the new plants and the water can be green. By year three, a well-designed natural pool runs clear throughout the swimming season with minimal intervention beyond plant management.

**Post NS-2** | `natural-swimming` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Natural swimming ponds have a fundamentally different relationship with ecology than chlorinated pools. A pool is a sterilized environment maintained by chemistry. A natural pond is a living system in dynamic balance ‚Äî one that supports damselflies, frogs, water beetles, beneficial bacteria, and dozens of aquatic plant species while remaining safe and pleasant to swim in. Some natural bathers find the presence of this life reassuring rather than alarming. You are swimming in a functioning ecosystem, not a chemically treated container. The design challenge is achieving that balance; the reward is a body of water that gets better rather than requiring more chemicals over time.

### off-grid-tech

**Post OG-1** | `off-grid-tech` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
The most resilient communication infrastructure for remote or disrupted communities is mesh networking ‚Äî a system where each node relays traffic for its neighbors, so there's no single point of failure. Commercial internet requires centralized infrastructure that fails when power goes down, fiber is cut, or providers stop service. A local mesh network built from open-source hardware (Meshtastic LoRa devices, Raspberry Pi nodes, or commercial mesh routers flashed with open firmware) can maintain local communication indefinitely with solar power. It doesn't reach the internet, but it reaches your neighbors, and in a crisis, that's what matters.

**Post OG-2** | `off-grid-tech` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
12V DC systems are more efficient for off-grid power than 120V/240V AC systems for most everyday loads. LED lighting, USB charging, water pumps, fans, and many tools run natively on 12V or 24V. Converting solar panel output to AC and then back to DC (which most electronics do internally) loses 15-20% of the energy to conversion inefficiency. A 12V native system skips that double conversion. The trade-off is that high-power loads (induction cooktops, power tools, workshop equipment) still need AC. Hybrid systems ‚Äî DC for everyday loads, inverter for high-power tools ‚Äî capture the efficiency of both approaches.

### Hemp Materials (North America)

**Post SRC-1** | `hempcrete` | ‚è≥ hold (hempcrete LoRA)
Sourcing hemp hurds (also called hemp shiv or hemp hurd) in North America has become dramatically easier since the 2018 Farm Bill legalized industrial hemp. The two most established suppliers for builder-grade hemp shiv are **Hempitecture** (Sun Valley, Idaho ‚Äî ships nationwide, also sells pre-blended hempcrete binder) and **American Lime Technology** (Vancouver, BC / US distribution ‚Äî also supplies the hydraulic lime). Both companies work directly with builders and can advise on mix ratios. If you're in the eastern US, smaller regional hemp processors have emerged since 2020; search the Hemp Industries Association directory for your nearest option.

**Post SRC-2** | `hempcrete` | ‚è≥ hold (hempcrete LoRA)
Hydraulic lime (NHL ‚Äî Natural Hydraulic Lime) is what makes hempcrete set. Not agricultural lime. Not lime putty. Not hydrated lime. NHL is produced by burning impure limestone containing clay minerals; the result sets both by carbonation and hydraulic reaction with water, making it suitable for damp environments and structural applications. The two grades used in hempcrete are NHL 2 (softer, more flexible, better for insulation-grade mixes) and NHL 3.5 (stronger set, better for load-bearing applications). In the US, **American Lime Technology** distributes St. Astier NHL from France. In Europe, St. Astier and Lias-Franken are the dominant suppliers. Do not substitute Portland cement ‚Äî it's incompatible with hempcrete's vapor management properties.

### Natural Plaster Materials

**Post SRC-3** | `natural-plasters` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Lime putty ‚Äî the gold standard finish plaster material ‚Äî is made by slaking quicklime in excess water and aging the resulting putty for months or years. The longer it ages, the smoother and more workable it becomes. Pre-bagged hydrated lime (what most hardware stores sell as "Type S lime") is a distant second ‚Äî it works but lacks the plasticity and workability of well-aged putty. For finish plaster work, source aged lime putty if you can. **Limeworks.us** in the eastern US and **Mike Wye & Associates** in the UK are known for quality lime putty and will advise on specification for your application.

**Post SRC-4** | `natural-plasters` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
For clay plaster, the most practical sourcing approach for most builders is test your site soil first. Dig down below topsoil, collect subsoil samples from several spots, and do a jar test: fill a jar with soil and water, shake, and let settle for 24 hours. The layers tell you your sand/silt/clay ratio. Clay-rich subsoil (deep red or orange layers common in much of the eastern US and Southwest) can be used directly in clay plaster with sand additions. If your site soil won't work, powdered clay in bags (available from pottery supply companies and some natural building suppliers) is the fallback. American Clay and Claylin are two US suppliers with builder-grade products.

### Seeds

**Post SRC-5** | `seed-saving` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
For open-pollinated seed that you can actually save from, the most established sources in the US are **Seed Savers Exchange** (Decorah, Iowa ‚Äî nonprofit with the largest heritage variety seed library in North America), **Fedco Seeds** (Maine ‚Äî worker-owned cooperative, extensive OP catalog, excellent regional adaptation notes), and **Baker Creek Heirloom Seeds** (Missouri ‚Äî large catalog, strong on unusual varieties). All three maintain and grow out their varieties rather than just reselling. When buying seed you plan to save, look for explicit "OP" or "heirloom" designation and avoid anything marked "F1" ‚Äî those won't breed true.

**Post SRC-6** | `seed-saving` | ‚úÖ existing circle image
Community seed libraries are a legal alternative to commercial seed companies for accessing locally adapted varieties. Most public libraries with a seed library will loan seeds from your region, often varieties that have been grown out and selected locally for years. You borrow, grow, save seed, and return the saved seed at the end of the season. The library's collection grows and improves over time through the combined selection work of hundreds of gardeners. The **Seed Library of Los Angeles**, the **Richmond Grows Seed Lending Library** in California, and hundreds of others operate this model. If your library doesn't have one, starting one costs almost nothing.
