The platform

Simple by design.
Solid by practice.

Most coordination tools make you feel like you're managing tickets, not building community. Earthback is built around the way good projects actually work — conversations first, commitments second, work together.

The basics

Three things you need.
Nothing you don't.

Everything in Earthback is built from three simple ideas. Once you understand them, you understand the whole platform.

◎

Circles

Your neighborhood inside Earthback. You join circles based on what you care about — natural structures, off-grid systems, food, mutual aid. Your circles shape your feed so you see what actually matters to you.

◈

Feed

What's happening in your circles right now. Projects forming, skills shared, resources offered, completions celebrated. Everything in your feed comes from the communities you joined.

◇

Projects

Where the actual work lives. A project has a goal, a timeline, and a list of commitments — who's bringing what, and when. When it's done, it's archived. The record stays.

04
Make a real commitment

Not a vague "I'm interested." When you join a project, you make a specific commitment — what you're bringing, when you'll do it. Time, skills, or resources. All three have a place.

Specific by design: Earthback commitments have a what, a when, and a who. Vague interest doesn't become accountability. Specific commitments do.

05
Show up and do the work

Tasks and events are lightweight on purpose. A task is "dig the footer trench by Friday." An event is "build day, March 22, 8am." Updates flow back to your circle automatically, so people know what's happening without needing to follow up.

Right-sized: Earthback doesn't try to run your project. It just holds the shape of what you agreed to, and makes it visible to the people who need to see it.

06
Close the loop. Keep the record.

When a project wraps, you archive it. The record stays: what got built, who showed up, what was committed and what was delivered. Over time, this becomes your track record — and the community's memory.

Why archives matter: Most projects end and disappear. In Earthback, completed projects are permanent — searchable by future groups, visible in profiles, part of the network's knowledge base.

Common questions

Things people ask

Do I have to start a project to use Earthback?
No. You can join as a contributor — someone who brings skills or resources to other people's projects. Many of the most active Earthback members have never started a project themselves.
What if a project falls apart before it finishes?
Projects can be cancelled or put on hold. The record reflects what happened honestly — who committed, who showed up, and what the outcome was. That honesty is part of how trust works on Earthback.
Is there any cost?
The core platform is free. Earthback doesn't monetize your data or activity. Future premium features for large groups or organizations may exist, but the community coordination layer will always be free.
What counts as "green building"?
Broadly defined. Natural building, renewable energy, water stewardship, food systems, mutual aid — these are the pillars. But Earthback welcomes anyone curious about this work, whether you're hands-on or just learning. If what you care about gives something back to the land or the community, there's a place for you here.
Can I join multiple circles?
Yes, and many people do. Your feed shows everything from all your circles. If that gets noisy, you can set priorities or temporarily mute circles you're less active in.
How is this different from a Facebook group?
Earthback is purpose-built for coordination. Your feed is shaped by your circles, the projects track commitments and resources, and the archives keep a living record of what your community has accomplished. It's designed to help you get things done together.
