The mission

Building things together
is the oldest work there is.

Earthback exists because that work has gotten harder to organize, not easier — and because the tools people use to do it are mostly optimized for other people's interests.

"The problem isn't that people don't want to build things together. The problem is that nothing helps them find each other."

Earthback started from a simple observation: builders are isolated. The skills, materials, tools, and labor that communities need to become more resilient exist — they're just fragmented across people who can't easily locate or coordinate with each other.

How it started

A coordination problem
masquerading as a resource problem

Every person who has tried to organize a build day, a food co-op, a neighborhood greywater project, or a mutual aid network has felt it: the work of coordination is harder than the work itself. You're texting people who don't text back, managing a spreadsheet that's always out of date, or posting in a Facebook group where your message drowns in noise.

The skills exist. The materials are often available. The willing hands are there. What's missing is a place that makes it easy for all of them to find each other and organize around a specific, bounded goal.

Earthback is that place. Not a social network. Not a project management tool. A community platform built specifically for people who want to build things that are good for the places they live in.

The ethos is simple: constructive, not extractive. If what you're building gives more than it takes — to the land, to the community, to the future — Earthback is for you. The circles are organized around what people build. The feed shows what's actually happening. The projects hold commitments. The archives keep the memory.

What we believe

The principles Earthback is built on

01

Collective action is irreplaceable

Individual action is meaningful. Market-based solutions have their place. But the problems communities face — housing, food sovereignty, energy resilience, water stewardship — require sustained collective action at a scale that neither individuals nor markets handle well on their own.

02

Skills and resources already exist

Most communities don't lack the materials or the knowledge to become more resilient. They lack the coordination infrastructure to put what already exists to work. A builder who knows hemp-lime construction lives 20 miles from someone building with straw bale. They just haven't found each other.

03

Trust is built through track record

You can't download trust. It accumulates through repeated commitments, kept or explained. A platform that makes commitments visible — and that holds the record honestly when they're met or missed — does more for community trust than any identity verification system.

04

Completed work is the community's memory

Most projects end and disappear. The next group who tries to do the same thing starts from zero. Earthback's archive is a deliberate design choice: every completed project is permanent, searchable, and useful to future builders. The network's knowledge compounds over time.

05

The feed should serve the people in it

Algorithmic feeds optimize for engagement — which is not the same as usefulness or accuracy. Earthback uses a simple circle-based feed: you see what the people in your circles post. No algorithmic amplification, no engagement optimization, no content from outside your circles unless you ask for it.

06

Governance belongs to the community

Earthback is governed by its Core Constitution — a document written to keep the platform accountable to the people who use it, not to investors or advertisers. The community has standing to shape how the platform evolves. No VC term sheet will override that.

What Earthback is not

Some things we've decided
not to build.

Not this
A social network optimized for engagement

Earthback doesn't have a global feed, a trending section, follower counts, or likes. The platform is not trying to maximize the time you spend on it. It's trying to help you get something done and then go do it.

Not this
A marketplace

Earthback is not optimized for commercial transactions. There's no listing fee, no payment processing, no promoted content. Resources move between people based on commitment and community relationship, not price.

Not this
A project management tool

There are no Gantt charts, no ticket queues, no velocity metrics. Projects in Earthback have commitments, tasks, and events — the minimum structure needed to coordinate real work without making the coordination feel like a job.

Not this
A VC-backed growth machine

Earthback is not built to be acquired, to serve ads, or to grow as fast as possible. It's built to be useful to the people who use it, governed by them, and resilient enough to keep working for a long time. Growth should follow usefulness, not the other way around.

Governance

Built by the community.
Governed by it.

Earthback operates under a Core Constitution — a governing document that sets out the rights of community members, the limits of platform authority, and the principles that guide how the platform evolves. The constitution is not a terms-of-service agreement designed to protect the company. It's a compact designed to protect the community.

The core commitments the constitution makes: the platform will never optimize for engagement over usefulness, will never sell member data, will never introduce payment mechanisms that undermine community trust, and will not make major changes to community structure without community consent.

The constitution is a living document. As Earthback grows and the community encounters situations the founding document didn't anticipate, members have standing to propose amendments. Changes require a structured consent process — not a quiet update to the terms of service.

This is deliberate. The history of community platforms is a history of communities that built real relationships and real value on someone else's infrastructure, only to have the rules change under them. Earthback is trying to do something different: give the community real standing and real teeth, not just a feedback form.

Who we are

Builders building
for builders

Earthback is a small team with direct experience in the kinds of projects this platform is designed to support: natural building, off-grid systems, food production, mutual aid, and community organizing. We've felt the coordination problem from the inside.

We're not funded by investors. We're not trying to build a billion-dollar company. We're trying to build something that's genuinely useful to communities who want to work together toward a more resilient world — and to make it sustainable enough to keep doing that for a long time.

If that sounds like you, come join us. Earthback is free and open. Early members get direct access to us, their feedback shapes what we build next, and their projects become the first entries in the community's shared archive.
