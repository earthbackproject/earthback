Maria has been doing hemp-lime construction for three years, but she doesn't have enough hands for the next phase of her studio build. She posts in the Natural Building circle — not an ad, just a description of what she's doing, what she needs, and when.

In 48 hours, six people have responded. Three have direct hemp-lime experience. Two want to learn. One is offering a truck and mixer. Maria creates a project, adds an event (Saturday, 8am, Taos), and everyone commits specifically — what they're bringing, how many hours, what skill level.

Nine people show up. Three walls go up. The project archives with a full record. Maria's circle sees it. Future organizers find it when planning their own build day.

Tom knows that solar panels get cheaper when you buy in bulk, but he's only one household. He posts in the Solar & Off-Grid circle asking if anyone nearby is planning a system this year.

Three households respond — two in the same county, one 40 miles away but willing to coordinate. Together they negotiate a group purchase with an installer who would have quoted each of them 15% more individually. Each household commits their system specs, timeline, and budget contribution.

All four systems go in. The savings and installer contact become part of the circle's knowledge — the next group who organizes a bulk buy finds it and starts faster.

Three neighboring households on a shared property each needed a greywater system, but the cost and permitting was prohibitive alone. Through the Water Systems circle, they discovered their properties share a natural drainage gradient that makes a shared system feasible.

The project ran eight months across three phases: site assessment, permitting (which they split), and installation. Each household committed specific financial contributions, labor days, and ongoing maintenance responsibilities.

The archived project documents the design, permit process, installer, and division of maintenance — everything the next group attempting this in their county needs to start faster.

Food Forest

A group of landowners and renters wanted to plant food forests on their properties but didn't have the design knowledge to do it well alone. A permaculture designer in the circle offered to run a monthly cohort — not as paid consulting, but as a skill-share in exchange for labor on her own site.

Twelve people joined. Six months, one session per month, rotating through each site. By the end, four properties had detailed food forest designs. Everyone who participated had both given and received. Three participants went on to start their own cohorts.

Before the first hard freeze, a neighborhood organizer posted in the Mutual Aid circle: she knew of at least six households that couldn't reliably heat through winter. She was looking for wood donors, haulers, and connectors who could identify households that hadn't yet asked for help.

The response was immediate. Donors committed specific quantities. Haulers committed dates and vehicle capacity. The project grew to 31 participants — donors, haulers, and recipients alike. By December, 14 cord had moved. No household went cold.

The archived project became a template. The following winter the network ran it again, faster, with more reach — using last year's record as a starting point.

Forming a community land trust is one of the most complex collective projects imaginable — legal structure, governance, fundraising, site selection, long-term stewardship decisions — all requiring sustained coordination among people with different skills and timelines.

Seven people in the Community Housing circle began together. Eighteen months of phased milestones, committed contributors, and documents attached to project phases meant nothing got lost between members and meetings. Governance documents, bylaws, and meeting records lived inside the project, not in someone's email.

The archived project is now one of the most detailed in the network — a documented path for any group attempting something similar.
